# Anti-AI tells — how to write so the reader can't tell

This is the most important file in this skill. Read carefully.

The book-writer skill has access to all the structural moves of good writing — persona, voice, research, citation, outline, revision. None of that prevents the most damaging failure mode of AI writing: that it *reads as AI*. A reader who feels, even subliminally, that they are reading machine output won't finish the book. A reviewer rejects it. An editor rewrites it. A friend hands it back with a polite "this needs work" that they couldn't quite articulate.

This file is about what they couldn't articulate. It catalogues the recurring tells of AI-generated prose, why they happen, and how to write *against* them.

Read this file:
- At the start of every writing session, before drafting any chapter
- During every self-critique pass, as a checklist
- After completing each chapter, as a final sweep before saving status as `reviewed`

This is not optional. The cost of an AI tell in a published book is enormous; the cost of one extra read of this file before drafting is small.

---

## The principle

AI-generated prose, even when grammatically perfect and topically accurate, has a *texture* that careful readers detect. The texture is the cumulative effect of small patterns: structural symmetry, rhythmic uniformity, predictable transitions, hedged claims, generic specificity, lists of three, parallelism that lands too neatly, sentence-length variation that follows an even sine wave, and an absence of the small irrationalities that mark a human mind on the page.

Writing against the texture is not a style. It is a discipline. The discipline: at every level — paragraph, sentence, choice of word — actively *break* the patterns that AI defaults to. Not for the sake of breakage. Because human writing is *uneven* in ways AI is not, and the unevenness is what makes it feel human.

What follows is the catalogue. Each tell has a description, why AI does it, why human writers don't (or don't always), a counter-move, and a test you can apply to your own draft.

## Tell 1 — Structural over-symmetry

**What it looks like.** Three chapters with three sections each. Four subsystems with four discipline-practices. Five reference engines getting five comparable sections. Bullet lists with carefully balanced item lengths. Sections that mirror each other at the paragraph level.

**Why AI does it.** Symmetry is locally easy. When the model produces a section about subsystem A and then needs to produce one about subsystem B, the path of least resistance is to mirror the structure. The cumulative effect across a chapter or book is a too-clean architecture.

**Why human writers don't.** A human covers what's worth covering at the length it deserves. The third subsystem might warrant three pages; the fourth might warrant half a page; the fifth might be a footnote. Asymmetry follows substance, not symmetry.

**Counter-move.** Before starting a section that will parallel an earlier one, ask: does this topic actually deserve the same depth? If less, shorten it deliberately. If more, expand it deliberately. Vary depths visibly. Sometimes the right answer is a single paragraph dispatching a topic the previous section spent three pages on.

**Test.** Look at your section lengths. Are they within 20% of each other? That is a tell. Real chapters have section lengths varying by 3x or more.

## Tell 2 — Triadic constructions everywhere

**What it looks like.** "Engines fail to evolve, fail to support the team's needs, fail to ship the game." "It's clear, concise, and concrete." "I read, I revised, I rewrote." Lists of three. Adjectives in threes. Verbs in threes.

**Why AI does it.** The rhetorical triad is the default training-data move for emphasis. Grammatically clean, sounds authoritative, feels rhythmically complete. AI defaults to it constantly.

**Why human writers don't (always).** Humans use triads for emphasis when emphasis is wanted. They also use pairs, single emphatic items, and lists of seven. They use a triad and immediately follow it with something irregular to break the cadence.

**Counter-move.** When you find yourself writing a triad, stop. Would two work? Would five? Would one stark item work better? Use triads sparingly, never two in the same paragraph. When you do use one, follow it with a sentence that breaks the rhythm — short, asymmetric, syntactically different.

**Test.** Count your triads per page. More than two per page is a tell.

## Tell 3 — The closing flourish

**What it looks like.** A chapter or section ends with a sentence designed to land. "The seams are where you spend your team." "Hold them well." "And that, more than anything, is the lesson." A short, parallel, aphoristic closer.

**Why AI does it.** The model has learned that sections "should" end emphatically. The closing flourish is the move it reaches for.

**Why human writers don't (always).** Many chapters end mid-thought, on a quiet observation, on a question, on a transition that is just a transition. Chapters that always end on a flourish feel manufactured.

**Counter-move.** For at least half your chapters, end without flourish. End on a parenthetical aside. End on a "we'll come back to this." End on an admission. End on a partial thought the next chapter completes. Save the flourish for one or two chapters per book where you want the reader to feel it.

**Test.** If every chapter's last sentence could be quoted in isolation as a tagline, you have a problem. Real books have chapters that end with sentences that need their context.

## Tell 4 — Hedged universal claims

**What it looks like.** "Most engines do this." "Many readers will find this useful." "In my experience, this generally works." "Some teams take a different approach." Claims that are technically defensible because they are vague.

**Why AI does it.** The model is trained to avoid overconfidence and to qualify claims. The result is a prose that hedges everything and commits to nothing.

**Why human writers don't (always).** Authors with expertise commit. They say "this is wrong" or "I do this" or "Carmack's approach was different." Hedging happens around specific claims that warrant it, not as a default register.

**Counter-move.** For every claim you write, ask: am I hedging because I'm genuinely uncertain, or because I'm being safe? If safety, commit. Use "this" instead of "this kind of thing." Use "I do" instead of "many practitioners do." Use "wrong" instead of "suboptimal." Save hedges for places where you genuinely don't know.

**Test.** Search your draft for "many", "most", "some", "often", "generally", "typically." Each is a candidate for replacement with a specific instance or a committed claim.

## Tell 5 — Generic specificity

**What it looks like.** "Early in my career, I encountered a bug..." "I worked on a project that..." "A team I was part of..." Specifics that are specific in form but generic in content. The "specifics" could fit any career.

**Why AI does it.** The model can produce the *shape* of an anecdote — temporal markers, first-person framing, a moral — without specific texture. Real specificity (which studio, which year, which game, which name) requires either real knowledge or invention; AI is wisely cautious about the latter.

**Why human writers don't (always).** Authors writing as themselves use real specifics, with disclaimers where needed. "At a studio I won't name, in 2018." "On a project that shipped — barely — in the late 2010s." The disclaimer is part of the specificity.

**Counter-move.** When the persona is fictional, decide on specifics in the persona file and use them consistently. Don't write "early in my career" — write "in 2009, six months into the studio." Don't write "a project I worked on" — write "the project that became [name], though my work was on the cancelled prequel." Even fictional specifics, when specific, read as real. Generic specifics read as AI.

When real specifics would breach confidentiality, use the form: specific frame + named disclaimer. "At a studio I won't name. The bug was in the asset streaming system. The year was around 2017." This is what real authors do.

**Test.** Search your anecdotes for "early in my career", "a project I worked on", "a team I was part of", "I once". These are the placeholders where AI defaults when it can't commit to specifics. Replace them with specifics or disclaimer-wrapped specifics.

## Tell 6 — The "not X but Y" construction

**What it looks like.** "It is not a metaphor; it is a description." "Not because it's wrong, but because it's incomplete." "This is not optimization, it is architecture." Negation followed by correction.

**Why AI does it.** A clean rhetorical move that creates the appearance of nuance. The model has learned that good writing is "nuanced," and "not X but Y" is a fast nuance generator.

**Why human writers don't (always).** Used once or twice per chapter, fine. Used five times, it becomes a mannerism. Human writers vary their negation patterns and often skip the correction entirely — they say what is, without first saying what isn't.

**Counter-move.** Limit "not X but Y" to one or two per chapter, only when the negation actually adds something. For most claims, just state Y.

**Test.** Count "not... but" constructions per chapter. More than three is a tell.

## Tell 7 — Sentence-length sine wave

**What it looks like.** Long sentence, medium sentence, short sentence. Long, medium, short. The rhythm is too regular. Even when sentences are well-constructed individually, the cumulative cadence is hypnotic in a way that signals machine-generated.

**Why AI does it.** The model has learned that "varied sentence length" marks good prose. So it varies — but it varies smoothly, in a regular pattern.

**Why human writers don't (always).** Real sentence-length variation is jagged. Three short sentences in a row, then one long, then a fragment, then another long, then a sentence and a half about what someone said, then a single word. The pattern is irregular, sometimes ugly, often surprising.

**Counter-move.** Read your paragraphs aloud and listen for the cadence. If it has a sing-song quality, break the pattern. Put two long sentences together. Put a single short sentence by itself between two medium ones. Use a fragment. Don't.

**Test.** Plot your sentence lengths across a paragraph. If they alternate predictably, that is a tell.

## Tell 8 — Em dashes used as a rhythm device

**What it looks like.** "The simulation — that is, the part of the engine that updates state — calls into the renderer, which — and this is key — owns its own timeline." Em dashes everywhere, used to inject parenthetical commentary, asides, and rhythmic variation.

**Why AI does it.** The em dash is versatile and almost always grammatically defensible. Many AI texts have one em dash per paragraph or more.

**Why human writers don't (as much).** Skilled authors use em dashes sparingly because the punctuation creates a particular rhythm. Heavy em-dash use makes prose feel breathless. Real prose typically has fewer — perhaps one every two or three paragraphs.

**Counter-move.** Limit em dashes to genuine parentheticals or genuine emphatic breaks. For asides, consider commas. For corrections, consider new sentences. For emphasis, consider just stating the thing emphatically. After drafting, count em dashes and remove half.

**Test.** More than two em dashes per paragraph is a tell. More than ten per page is almost always a tell.

## Tell 9 — Connector tics — "Indeed", "Thus", "Furthermore", "Moreover"

**What it looks like.** Paragraphs beginning with "Indeed," "Thus," "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Notably." Connectors that signal logical relationship without genuinely deepening it.

**Why AI does it.** Connectors are training-data-rich. They make prose feel coherent. They are the model's default way of moving from one paragraph to the next.

**Why human writers don't (often).** Real prose moves between paragraphs by topic, not by connector. "And" and "But" at sentence starts are common, sparingly. "Indeed" and "Thus" and "Moreover" appear in academic papers more than in trade non-fiction, and even there sparingly.

**Counter-move.** Strip "Indeed," "Thus," "Furthermore," "Moreover" from your draft entirely. If a paragraph needs a connector, "And" or "But" works. Often no connector is best — the topic itself bridges.

**Test.** Search your draft for these words at paragraph or sentence starts. Each is a candidate for deletion.

## Tell 10 — The "it's worth noting" filler

**What it looks like.** "It's worth noting that..." "It's important to mention..." "One thing to keep in mind..." "Interestingly..." Phrases that flag what follows as worth attention rather than just stating it.

**Why AI does it.** The model has learned that good writing draws attention to important points. Flagging is the most direct way to draw attention. The cumulative effect is prose that constantly tells the reader what's important rather than just being important.

**Why human writers don't.** Skilled authors trust their own selection. If they wrote it down, it's worth noting; the reader assumes that. Flagging insults the reader by suggesting they might miss what's important without help.

**Counter-move.** Strip these phrases entirely. The claim that follows them stands fine on its own. "It's worth noting that the cancelled project taught me about seams" becomes "The cancelled project taught me about seams." Stronger every time.

**Test.** Search for "it's worth noting", "it's important", "one thing to", "interestingly", "notably". Each is removable, almost always.

## Tell 11 — Symmetric sentence pairs that resolve too neatly

**What it looks like.** "The first costs you time. The second costs you trust." "Some engines ship; some don't." "What they say matters; what they do matters more." Two clauses balanced against each other, with a closing parallel that lands as a punchline.

**Why AI does it.** Parallelism is rhetorically satisfying. The model has learned that good writing has rhetorical force, and balanced pairs are a fast way to get force.

**Why human writers don't (always).** Human writers use balanced pairs occasionally. They also write asymmetric sentences that say one thing and stop. The constant punch-line cadence reads as performance.

**Counter-move.** When you write a balanced pair, ask: does this rhythm earn itself, or am I performing? If performing, rewrite as a single direct claim. "The first costs you time. The second costs you trust" → "The second costs you trust, which is harder to recover than time."

**Test.** Count balanced pairs per page. More than three is a tell. More than one per paragraph is almost always one.

## Tell 12 — The summary paragraph that recaps what was just said

**What it looks like.** Section ends. A paragraph begins: "So we've seen that..." or "To recap..." or "What I've been arguing is...". The paragraph then restates, in slightly different words, what the previous three pages already said.

**Why AI does it.** The model has been trained on academic and instructional prose, where summaries are common. It defaults to producing them at section boundaries.

**Why human writers don't (often).** Trade non-fiction rarely recaps mid-chapter. The reader just read it; recapping insults them. Summaries belong at the end of long chapters, sparingly, and only when the chapter has actually been complex enough to need one.

**Counter-move.** Cut summary paragraphs unless the section is genuinely complex enough to warrant one. Trust your prose to have made its point. The next section can start without the previous section being recapped.

**Test.** Search for "So we've seen", "To recap", "In summary", "What I've been arguing". Each is a candidate for cutting.

---

## Tell 13 — Smooth scholarly transitions where a hard cut would be better

**What it looks like.** "Having established X, we now turn to Y." "With this in mind, let us consider Z." "Building on the previous section, we can now examine W." Transition sentences that explicitly walk the reader from one topic to the next.

**Why AI does it.** The model has learned that good writing is "well-organised," and explicit transitions are the most visible marker of organisation.

**Why human writers don't (always).** Real prose often moves between topics by hard cut. New section. New topic. Trust the section break to do the work. Smooth transitions read as filler when the section break would have communicated the same shift in zero words.

**Counter-move.** When you find yourself writing a transition sentence, ask: would a section break do this work alone? Often yes. Cut the transition; let the heading or blank line carry it.

**Test.** Look at the first sentence of each section. If many start with "Having", "With", "Building on", or similar transition phrases, the chapter has too many smooth transitions. Replace half with hard cuts.

## How to use this file during writing

**Before drafting a chapter.** Skim the thirteen tells. Especially refresh on the ones you've struggled with before (every author has favourites). The point is to enter the draft with the patterns in mind, so you write with them rather than discovering them in revision.

**During drafting.** Don't try to apply all thirteen at once. The draft will become tortured. Just write — but with the texture in your ear. If a triad slips in, fine. The revision pass will catch it.

**During the self-critique pass.** Walk through the file as a checklist. For each tell, scan the chapter for instances. Mark candidates for revision. Don't try to remove all of them — some triads, some em dashes, some closing flourishes are fine. The goal is to thin them, not eliminate them.

**Specific revision moves to try.**

1. **Read the chapter aloud.** Sing-song cadence is unmistakable when you hear it. The eye misses what the ear catches.
2. **Run the search-and-replace test.** Search for each of the giveaway phrases ("it's worth noting", "moreover", "many", "in summary", "having established"). Each hit is a candidate.
3. **Diff against earlier chapters.** Do all your chapters end with a flourish? Do all your sections have three subsections? Symmetries across chapters are the hardest to see in any single chapter.
4. **Cut by 10–20%.** Most AI prose is too long. Cutting forces choices that reveal which sentences are genuine and which are filler.
5. **Replace one connective tissue paragraph per section with a hard cut.** White space carries a lot of meaning. Trust it.

## What this file does not cover

This file is about texture — the cumulative feel of prose. It does not cover:

- **Voice consistency** — that's `writer-persona.md`
- **Structural craft** — that's `writing-craft.md` and `fiction-craft.md`
- **Factual rigour** — that's `research-protocol.md` and `fact-check.md`
- **Claims and citations** — that's `citation-styles.md`

A chapter can be perfect on all four of those and still read as AI because of texture. Texture is its own discipline.

### Things that look like AI tells but aren't

A few patterns get mistakenly flagged as AI tells. They are not.

**Absence of typos.** A finished book has no typos. Copy-editors catch them in the production passes that every published book goes through. A reader who sees a typo thinks "sloppy," not "human." Producing typo-free prose is what the skill should do; it is not a tell.

**Absence of grammatical errors.** Same reasoning. Edited prose is grammatical.

**Consistent terminology.** Style guides exist precisely to enforce consistency across a book. A chapter that uses one term for one concept throughout is well-edited, not machine-generated.

**Absence of factual mistakes.** Fact-checking is a production phase. A chapter that survives it has no remaining mistakes. That's a quality marker, not an AI tell.

**Polished sentences.** Sentences in published prose have been revised, sometimes many times. They should read polished.

The tells in this file are about *patterns of choice* that AI systematically makes — symmetry, triads, hedging, generic specificity, surface uniformity. Those patterns survive copy-editing and fact-checking unchanged, because they're not errors. They're stylistic defaults. That's what makes them tells, and that's why they're worth a dedicated discipline.

## A final note on the test

The hardest test, and the one that finally matters: **show your work to a careful human reader and ask them to mark places where the prose feels off.** Don't tell them what you're testing for. Just ask them to mark anywhere they paused, re-read, or felt the prose was working too hard. Their marks will cluster on AI tells you missed.

This test is expensive — it requires a reader's time. Use it on at least one chapter per book, ideally early enough that the patterns can be corrected before they spread to other chapters. A reader can give you in twenty minutes what no automated check can.

The thirteen tells in this file are an attempt to build that reader's instinct into the skill itself. They cover most of what a careful human reader catches. They do not cover all of it. When you can, get a reader.
