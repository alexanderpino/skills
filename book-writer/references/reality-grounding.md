# Reality grounding — borrowing from real life, history, and myth

## Why this discipline exists

Fiction that works almost always borrows from reality. Not as decoration, not as Easter eggs, but as **load-bearing structure**. The borrowing happens at four scales:

- **Macro** — political systems, wars, plagues, migrations, religious institutions, technological revolutions. The big patterns of history that give a story its weight.
- **Micro** — daily life, small frustrations, the way people actually argue, cook, mourn, get bored, fall asleep. The texture that makes characters feel like people instead of plot-functions.
- **The dark and the strange** — specific case studies of human extremity, from named historical monsters to obscure incidents. Material that gives villains and crises specific menace instead of generic menace.
- **Mythological and folkloric** — the stories that human cultures have told about themselves for centuries or millennia. Designed to be retold; carry archetypal resonance; provide worldbuilding that does not need to be built.

The discipline is the same at all four scales: **borrow from real materials, then choose against the templates that AI generation produces by default.**

That second clause is the heart of this file. Read it twice.

---

## The template problem — the master discipline

AI generation has read an enormous amount of fiction, history, mythology, and human-life writing. It can produce competent passages at all four scales. The problem is not raw material. The problem is **defaults**.

When you ask AI for "a character grieving while cooking," the model has read hundreds of grief-while-cooking scenes. It will average them. The result is plausible — tea is made, the kettle whistles, a hand pauses on a cupboard, light slants through a window — and the result is a *template*. Every clause sits inside the genre-average for that situation. The reader who reads attentively feels the smoothness without being able to name it.

A real writer working on the same scene chooses *against* the template. Not by adding random eccentricity, but by knowing the specific person whose scene this is and choosing what *that person* would do, even when the choice is not the most evocative. *De IJzeren Vloot*'s Valis cooking after Servia's death would not stand at a window with the kettle whistling. He might wash the same plate three times and not notice. He might eat standing at the counter because sitting at the table without her there is the version he has not yet found a way to do. The choice is small and specific. It is a choice against the ten plausible options the template would generate.

This applies at all four scales:

- **Macro:** AI defaults to "evil empire rises" with template-iconography. The writer chooses *which* evil empire's specific mechanism to borrow from. (Star Wars works because the Reich's specific machinery is borrowed, not "evil empire"-mechanics.)
- **Micro:** AI defaults to evocative-but-generic small detail. The writer chooses the specific tic of *this* character, often a tic that contradicts the evocative default.
- **Dark:** AI defaults to theatrical menace. The writer chooses the specific banality of a real case, often a banality that is more frightening than the theatrical version.
- **Mythological:** AI defaults to surface iconography (Odin, Thor, Loki appearing as named characters). The writer chooses the *structure* of the mythology — its moral assumptions, its sense of fate — and lets the iconography stay implicit.

The discipline of choosing against the template is what separates genre-competent prose from prose that lands. The skill should hold this principle as the master discipline of reality grounding.

---

## When this discipline applies

Almost all fiction. Specifically:

- **Speculative fiction** (SF, fantasy, alternate history) — where the temptation to invent everything is strongest, and the result is a world that feels weightless
- **Historical fiction** — obviously
- **Literary fiction** with social or political content
- **Thrillers, mysteries, crime, horror** — where plausibility of motive and method requires research into actual cases
- **Domestic and relationship fiction** — where the texture of daily life carries the whole book
- **Mythological retellings, fantasy, mythopoeic literature** — where mythological grounding is the constitutive material

It applies less directly to:

- **Pure surrealism / fabulism** that explicitly rejects realist plausibility
- **Memoir** — where the ground is the writer's actual life and the discipline is honesty rather than borrowing

For most fiction projects, the discipline applies in some combination of the four scales. **The skill should propose this conversation during Phase 2 (Preparation) as a default, not wait for the user to ask.**

## Scale 1 — Macro: history, politics, civilisation

### What to borrow

Big patterns. The structural mechanics of how power, belief, violence, migration, knowledge actually move in human societies.

- **The corruption of a republic** — late Roman republic into principate, Weimar to Reich, Russia 1917-1921. The specific machinery of how legitimate institutions produce illegitimate outcomes.
- **Cultural collision** — Cortés meeting the Mexica, Lindisfarne and the Vikings, the Crusader States, the Columbian exchange. The texture of mutual non-translation.
- **Disasters and what cultures do with them** — 1918 influenza (forgotten because it did not fit narrative), Black Death (remembered into religious revolution), Chernobyl (politically transformative), the Holocaust (the central reference point of modern moral imagination).
- **Religious schism and the politics of belief** — Late Antique Christianity, the Reformation, Sufi traditions and their persecution, Buddhist sectarian conflicts.
- **Migration and refugee dynamics** — Migration Period, Partition of India, Vietnamese boat people, post-2015 Syria.
- **Knowledge traditions and their loss** — Royal Society, Library of Alexandria (its slow decay rather than the dramatic burning), medieval guilds, Manhattan Project, Bell Labs.
- **Colonial extraction and resistance** — Belgian Congo, the Raj, Dutch East Indies, Algeria.

### Done well

*Star Wars*: Reichstagbrand-style engineered emergency, parade-state aesthetics. The political physics is correct; the iconography is unmistakable to anyone who knows.

*Dune*: Bedouin tribal organisation, Saudi oil politics, Mahdi tradition, Ottoman court intrigue. The texture of Arrakis is *correct*.

*A Song of Ice and Fire*: Wars of the Roses, medieval North Sea world for the Starks, Mediterranean medieval for the southern kingdoms, the Mongol invasion for the Dothraki.

*Foundation*: Gibbon's *Decline and Fall*, almost wholesale.

### Done badly

A fantasy that has "an evil empire" without committing to *which* evil empire. The result reads as generic-fantasy-empire. The writer thinks they are being free; they are being weightless. Tolkien's Mordor works because it is rooted in WWI Western Front imagery (Tolkien fought there) plus Anglo-Saxon eschatology. Generic-Mordor would not.

The template-failure: when no specific historical case is borrowed, AI defaults to genre-empire-with-genre-features. Pick a real case. Borrow its mechanism.

## Scale 2 — Micro: daily life, small things, lived texture

### Why this scale is hard

Micro-scale lived texture is *available* in published fiction — Tsjechov, Carver, Knausgård, Robinson, Munro, Updike, Tolstoy at his most domestic. The corpus exists. AI has read it.

The failure mode is not that AI cannot generate small lived detail. The failure mode is that AI generates **the average** of the small lived detail it has read. A character makes tea, the steam rises, light comes through the window, hands cradle the cup. Each clause is plausible because each clause is the genre-mean of grief-while-cooking. The result reads as true and feels as template.

Real writers escape this by having a specific person in mind — a character built in enough detail that *this person* would not do the average thing. Carver's people sit at kitchen tables in particular silences that are not interchangeable with Munro's silences. Knausgård's father shaving is not anyone else's father shaving.

### What to ask the user

Not formal questions. Invitations to specify:

- "What is something specific your protagonist does that drives the people around them slightly mad — not as a major character flaw, just as a habit?"
- "What does your protagonist do when they are alone and not doing anything plot-relevant?"
- "What is a small daily ritual that has changed for them since the inciting event?"
- "Is there something you have noticed in your own life this week — a small pointless thing — that you could give to a character?"

The questions pull *observed* detail out of the user. The character's habits should be specific enough that the user can describe them in two sentences.

### How to use it on the page

One moment of texture-from-life per scene is usually enough. More becomes texture-overload. The moment should be one the writer has actually experienced, witnessed, or imagined with enough specificity to feel non-template, then twisted to fit the scene.

For fantasy and SF, this is *especially* important. The temptation is to invent the texture along with the world. Ground the texture in observed life, then transpose. A medieval-fantasy character is bored at a banquet in the same way you are bored at a wedding. Use that.

### The discipline of choosing against the template

When writing a small moment, generate three or four template-options in your head. The first version that comes to mind is almost always the template. Then ask:

- Would *this character specifically* do this? Or is this a generic-character-in-this-situation?
- Is there a small specific thing — a habit, a tic, an off-key choice — that would be true of this person and not of the template?
- What if I left out the most evocative element? (The kettle whistling. The light through the window. The hand pausing.) What if it were drier than the template wants?

The dryness is sometimes the answer. Carver writes drier than the template wants. The drama arrives because it has no decoration to compete with.

### A worked example

Template version of "Valis cooks after Servia's death":

> The kitchen was quiet. He set the kettle on the stove and watched the steam curl up into the late-afternoon light. Servia had loved the late afternoon. He stood there for a long time, not moving, until the kettle began to whistle and he reached out for it without thinking.

Every clause is the genre-mean. Plausible. Smooth. Template.

Choosing against the template:

> He had washed the same plate three times before he noticed. He set it down and went to the window and stood looking at nothing. The pan was burning. He could smell it. He went back to the stove and turned it off and ate what was in the pan standing up at the counter, because sitting down at the table by himself was a thing he had not yet found a way to do.

Different mechanism. Forgetting (washing the plate three times) replaces evocation (the kettle, the light). The unexamined small failure (the pan burning) replaces the literary pause. The eating-standing-up makes the absence specific. Servia is more present here, by being more absent, than she was in the template version where her name appeared.

The second version is also imperfect. A real writer would still revise it. But it is closer to choosing against the template.

## Scale 3 — The dark and the strange

### What to borrow

Specific real cases of human extremity. The named ones and the obscure ones.

**Named historical figures whose specific qualities transfer:**

- **Vlad III "the Impaler"** — political ruthlessness behind the legend, theatrical cruelty as deterrent statecraft, the actual military context against the Ottomans. Stoker took surface only; the real Vlad is more useful.
- **Ed Gein** — the lonely Wisconsin caretaker who was also a desecrator, the *absence* of theatrical menace, the small farmhouse, the mother. Used in *Psycho* (the mother), *Texas Chainsaw* (the surface), *Silence of the Lambs* (the skinning, the psychology). Each takes different elements.
- **Ted Bundy** — the intelligent charm, the calculated normality, the way he could be in a room and not be noticed for what he was.
- **Elizabeth Báthory** — aristocratic insulation, class power enabling atrocity, the question of how much was politically motivated invention.
- **John Wayne Gacy** — public face (clown, community organiser) and private one (basement). The split self.
- **Aileen Wuornos** — the female killer, the victim-becomes-perpetrator backstory that resists easy moral framing.
- **Andrei Chikatilo** — the Soviet context, the inattentive state failing to recognise a serial killer for years.
- **Adolf Eichmann** — Arendt's "banality of evil" — the bureaucrat at his desk. The most useful single concept for writing about institutional atrocity.
- **The Zodiac** — the unsolved case, the ciphers, the ongoing failure to know.

**Atrocities and their specific texture:**

- **The Holocaust** — bureaucratic banality, the camp guards' family lives, rail timetables, the slow precedent-by-precedent escalation. *Maus*, *The Reader*, *HHhH*.
- **The Rwandan genocide** — radio broadcasts, neighbour-against-neighbour, the speed (100 days). The terrifying ordinariness of the perpetrators.
- **The Khmer Rouge** — year-zero ideology producing specific atrocity, the targeting of intellectuals, the empty cities.
- **The Atlantic slave trade** — specific economics, the shipboard resistance and suicides, the Middle Passage.
- **Stalinism** — the denunciation system, the disappearances, the family members signing the papers.
- **The Belgian Congo under Leopold** — the rubber quotas, the severed hands.

**Specific non-monstrous strangenesses:**

- The dancing plague of 1518 in Strasbourg
- The Tunguska event of 1908
- The Voynich manuscript
- The Dyatlov Pass incident
- The disappearance of Roanoke colony
- The Mary Celeste
- The Tamám Shud case
- The Wow! signal
- Hessdalen lights

### What this scale gives a story

**Specific texture of evil.** Real evil is mostly bureaucratic, mostly banal, sometimes charming, often mistakable for normalcy. AI's default is theatrical menace. The discipline is to choose against the theatrical and pick the banal-and-specific. *Silence of the Lambs* gets this from Gein. *American Psycho* gets it from Wall Street culture plus Bundy's specific charm.

**Specific texture of strangeness.** The world has had real events that resist explanation. Borrowing the *quality* of real unexplained events lets fiction have unexplained things without feeling lazy. Þagnir in *De IJzeren Vloot* borrows from the dancing plague more usefully than from invented horror tropes.

**Moral ballast.** A villain who borrows specifically from a real case has weight that an invented villain rarely achieves. The reader's discomfort is real because the reader knows something like this happened.

### Choosing against the template at this scale

AI defaults to:
- Theatrical villains who deliver speeches about their philosophy
- Atrocities depicted at climactic moments rather than in their bureaucratic preparation
- Strangeness that resolves to a clear cause by the end
- Evil that announces itself

Real writers default to:
- Villains who are charming, ordinary, unmemorable until you know
- Atrocities depicted in their ordinary preparation — the timetable, the meeting, the form filled in
- Strangeness that does not resolve, that the work has the courage to leave unresolved
- Evil that does not announce itself, that the reader has to recognise

### Ethics

Real darkness has real ethical weight:

- **Do not name living perpetrators** in fiction.
- **Be cautious with named victims**, especially recent ones with surviving family.
- **Do not glamorise.** The discipline of borrowing from real cases is to *deflate* theatrical evil, not enhance it.
- **Acknowledge sources** in author's notes for serious work.
- **Avoid one-to-one lifting** of real cases as plot. Combine, twist, transform.

## Scale 4 — Mythological and folkloric

### Why this scale is uniquely rich

Three things make myth different from other source material:

**Myths are designed to be retold.** *Antigone* exists in twenty legitimate versions. The Arthuriad has been retold for fifteen centuries and counting. A writer borrowing from myth is participating in a tradition where retelling is the norm. This is unlike borrowing from Ed Gein.

**Myths carry archetypal resonance.** Certain story patterns work cross-culturally because they touch deep human structures: descent and return, the son who must kill the father, the twin who is also the self, the sacrifice that restores the community, the trickster who disrupts the order, the king who wounds the land. Fiction that touches these structures gets emotional weight without having to build it from scratch.

**Myths supply worldbuilding without building.** If you borrow Yggdrasil as world-tree, you do not have to explain why a world-tree works. The mythology has done the work over millennia.

### Major mythological traditions and what they offer

**Greek and Roman.** The most over-used source in Western fiction; therefore the hardest to borrow without cliché. Specific borrowings still work: Ovid's transformation logic (Madeline Miller's *Circe*), Homeric battle-rhythm (most epic-fantasy combat owes Homer something), Greek tragedy's fate-versus-character machinery still dominates serious literary tragedy. Useful elements: the *hubris-nemesis* arc, the chorus as collective conscience, *anagnorisis* (the recognition scene), the descent to the underworld.

**Norse and Germanic.** Dominant in fantasy through Tolkien, who used it skilfully. The doom-tone — even gods will fall — gives moral weight Greek myth lacks. Tolkien's debt: Beren and Lúthien from Orpheus and the Edda; Túrin from Kullervo; the moral atmosphere of Middle-earth from Norse acceptance that defeat is possible even for the good. Useful elements: the oath that binds absolutely, the named weapon with its own history, the funeral as ship or pyre, hospitality as sacrament, *wyrd* / fate that even gods cannot escape.

**Celtic** (Irish, Welsh, Scottish, Gallic). The Mabinogion, the Ulster Cycle, the Fenian Cycle. Fiercer and stranger than Norse — full of transformations, otherworld journeys, riddle-contests, *geasa* (taboo-bonds). Underused outside specifically Irish/Welsh writers. Useful elements: the geis (a personal taboo whose violation produces tragedy), the otherworld accessed through specific places, the riddle as binding contest, the woman-of-power whose name must not be spoken.

**Biblical and Abrahamic** (Jewish, Christian, Islamic). The largest source of moral imagery in Western literature. Cain and Abel, Job, the Exodus, the Crucifixion, the Apocalypse. McCarthy borrows from the King James prose register; Dostoevsky from Christian moral architecture; Phil Pullman from inverted Christian imagery. Useful elements: covenant as binding instrument, the prophet whose role is to be unheard, the wandering punishment, the resurrection-after-three-days structure, the apocalyptic register where personal failures echo cosmic ones.

**Egyptian.** Less used but rich. The afterlife as judgment with weighing of the heart. Osiris's death and resurrection (the original dying-and-rising god; Christ borrowed from this lineage). Isis as world-mother. Set as legitimate-but-troubling chaos. Useful elements: the heart-weighing, the underworld trials, the complex ma'at-versus-chaos relationship.

**Mesopotamian.** *Gilgamesh* — the oldest preserved long narrative. The friendship plot, the quest for immortality and its failure, the flood narrative (which the Bible borrowed from). Inanna's descent. Useful elements: friendship-as-civilising-force (Gilgamesh and Enkidu), the failed quest, the lament for the city.

**Hindu.** *Mahabharata* and *Ramayana*. Vast scale, multi-generational, with cosmic dharma-questions running underneath. The Bhagavad Gita as moral-decision moment within a battle. Cyclical cosmology (yugas, eternal return). Zelazny borrowed for *Lord of Light*; Delany for various works. Useful elements: moral debate before battle, dharma as obligation rather than wish, the warrior who must kill kinsmen for the right reasons, avatar (god-in-human-form) tradition.

**Chinese.** *Journey to the West* (Sun Wukong / Monkey King), *Romance of the Three Kingdoms*, *Water Margin*. Daoist immortals, eight-immortals tradition, the bureaucracy of heaven mirroring earthly bureaucracy. Borrowed by the Wachowskis for *The Matrix*. Useful elements: the trickster who serves the order, the bureaucratic underworld, the master-disciple relationship as cosmic pattern.

**Japanese.** Shinto kami-tradition (eight-million gods of place), Buddhist overlay producing specific syncretism, Heian-court aesthetics (*mono no aware*, the pathos of things). Yokai and ghost traditions. Useful elements: place-spirits, aesthetics of impermanence, the specifically female and specifically wronged vengeful ghost (*yūrei*), the cherry-blossom register.

**Yoruba and West African.** The orisha tradition (Shango, Ogun, Yemoja, Eshu). Brought to the Americas through the Atlantic slave trade and surviving in Santería, Candomblé, Vodou. Octavia Butler borrowed; Tomi Adeyemi explicitly draws on this for *Children of Blood and Bone*; Toni Morrison's metaphysics in *Beloved* has this register. Useful elements: orisha as personal patron-and-teacher, Eshu as truth-revealer, the moral weight of ancestry, the syncretism between African tradition and Catholic iconography in diaspora forms.

**Mesoamerican** (Maya, Mexica). The *Popol Vuh*, Mexica cyclical-destruction cosmology. Underused outside Latin American writers. Carlos Fuentes used Mexica; the Mayan world-tree is genuinely strange. Useful elements: the world-as-bloodgift cosmology, the calendar as cosmic engine, the hero-twins (Hunahpu and Xbalanque) as specifically dual, cyclical destruction-and-recreation.

**Indigenous American** (North America). Trickster traditions (Coyote, Raven), the specific First Nations and individual tribal traditions (Lakota, Navajo, Hopi, Iroquois — these are not interchangeable). Louise Erdrich has built a body of work on Anishinaabe tradition specifically. Useful elements: trickster as creator (different from European trickster), specifically place-rooted nature of these traditions (a generic "Native American mythology" is the wrong way to borrow; pick a tradition), the sacredness of language and stories as living entities.

**Polynesian and Oceanic.** Maui (the Polynesian, not the Disney version), navigation-by-stars tradition that is itself partly mythologised. Tahitian, Hawaiian, Maori, Samoan cosmologies are not interchangeable. Useful elements: the named ancestor as continuing presence, journey-by-star as both literal and mythic, the *tapu* (taboo) system.

**Slavic.** Baba Yaga, Koschei the Deathless, the firebird, the rusalka. Slavic folktale's moral atmosphere is fiercer and more ambiguous than Western European. Catherynne Valente has built a career partly on this; Naomi Novik dipped in for *Spinning Silver*. Useful elements: witch-grandmother as both threat and helper, soul-hidden-outside-the-body, the journey through forests where the trees themselves are dangerous.

**Persian / Zoroastrian.** Cosmic dualism of Ahura Mazda and Angra Mainyu, the *Shahnameh*'s dynastic epic, the *farr* (royal glory). Affected through Manichaean and Sufi inheritance. Influences medieval Christian dualism more than is often recognised. Useful elements: cosmic dualism that is *not* reducible to good-vs-evil (it is more ordering-vs-chaos), the king whose legitimacy depends on cosmic favour, the Persian poetic tradition.

### Choosing against the template at this scale

AI defaults to:
- **Surface iconography from the most-used traditions** (Greek, Norse, Christian). Odin, Thor, Zeus, Hades, the Devil, angels with white wings.
- **Generic fantasy-mythology** that combines elements without coherent cultural logic — the Tolkien-pastiche failure where you have elves and dragons and dwarves and "the gods" without thinking about what cosmology produces them.
- **One-to-one borrowing** — a character who *is* Loki, a journey that *is* the Odyssey.
- **The most-told versions.** Of the Odyssey, the Homeric. Of Arthur, the Malory. Of Norse, the Disney-Marvel.

Real writers default to:
- **Structural borrowing**, not iconographic. Tolkien took Norse moral atmosphere; he did not put Odin in his book. The doom-tone is the borrowing. The named characters are invented.
- **Less-told versions.** The Welsh Arthuriad before Malory. The Sumerian Inanna instead of the Greek Persephone. Specific tribal traditions instead of generic indigenous-mythology.
- **Twisted and combined sources.** Tolkien combines Norse, Anglo-Saxon, Finnish, Welsh, plus Germanic linguistic reconstruction, plus Catholic moral framework. The result is none of those and is recognisably all of them.
- **Implicit rather than explicit.** Madeline Miller's *Circe* names Circe; Le Guin's Earthsea borrows from Daoism without ever naming Daoist concepts. Both work; the second is harder.

### The persona credible-knowledge constraint

A writer-persona has a specific cultural background, education, reading history. The mythological traditions they can borrow from credibly are constrained by what they actually know.

For Marten Holvast (Dutch software architect, 39, gymnasium-educated): credible deep knowledge of Greek, Roman, Norse, biblical mythology. Reasonable surface knowledge of Celtic and Egyptian. Probably no deep knowledge of Hindu, Chinese, Yoruba, or indigenous American — which means borrowing from those traditions for *De IJzeren Vloot* would require either real research or the borrowing risks shallow pastiche.

The skill should cross-check proposed mythological borrowings against persona knowledge. If the user wants a borrowing from a tradition the persona does not know:
- Adjust the persona to include relevant graduate study or research interest
- Schedule research time and treat the borrowing as Phase-2 research rather than intuitive use
- Pick a different tradition the persona credibly knows

This applies *especially* sharply to non-Western traditions. A Western writer borrowing from Yoruba or Maya tradition without real knowledge produces work that practitioners and scholars from those traditions will reject as appropriation. Better a deep borrowing from a tradition the persona has lived inside than a shallow borrowing from one they have not.

### Documentation

For each mythological borrowing, document in the project's `notes/reality-grounding.md`:
- Which tradition (specific — "Norse Edda" not "Norse mythology")
- What is being borrowed at the structural level (moral atmosphere, fate-mechanism, cosmological pattern)
- What is being borrowed at the iconographic level, if anything (specific names, objects, images)
- What specifically is *not* being borrowed (this prevents drift toward pastiche)
- The depth of borrowing (subtle / visible to literate reader / declared)
- Persona-knowledge basis (gymnasium-level, lived experience, research)

## The discipline of twisting, combining, parodying

This is what makes borrowing into fiction at all four scales.

### Twisting

Take a real source and shift one or more variables. What can be twisted: setting (same psychological structure, different time and place), scale (small case becomes large), outcome (real ended one way, fiction ends another), perspective (viewed from outside vs inside), motive (replaced with one that better serves theme), gender/age/class (shifted to make dynamic legible or borrowing less obvious).

The twist must be **purposeful**. Random twisting is just disguise. A good twist makes the borrowing serve the story's themes that the original did not serve.

### Combining

Take elements from two or more real sources and integrate them. Buffalo Bill in *Silence of the Lambs* combines Gein (skinning, Wisconsin farmhouse), Bundy (disguise as injured man to lure victims), and Gary Heidnick (kidnapping and basement). Tolkien combines Norse, Anglo-Saxon, Finnish, Welsh, Germanic linguistic reconstruction, and Catholic moral framework.

Combination produces work that is not any single source and feels weighted by all of them. Readers familiar with one source feel the weight; readers familiar with several feel an unsettling completeness. **The skill should encourage combination over single-source borrowing for major elements.**

### Parodying

The trickiest mode. Done well: *American Psycho*, *Catch-22*, *Dr Strangelove*. Done badly: stuff that is neither funny nor serious.

Parody requires that the writer fully understands the thing being parodied. Half-understood parody is just mocking. Bret Easton Ellis knew Wall Street culture intimately. Heller served in WWII.

The skill should be cautious about proposing parody unless the writer has specific evidence of mastery of the parodied material.

### When to twist a little, when to twist a lot

- **Twist a little** when borrowing is at the level of texture or single motif. A character has Bundy's specific charm — fine.
- **Twist a lot** when borrowing is at the level of full plot structure. If your novel's plot is essentially the Manson Family, twist substantially or you are writing about Manson with the names changed.

Rule of thumb: the larger the borrowing, the larger the twist must be.

## What the skill should do during Phase 2

Add to preparation, before world-building begins in detail:

1. **Identify the major dimensions** of the planned story for grounding at scales 1, 3, and 4. Walk through the inventory: politics, disaster, cultural collision, institutions, migration, knowledge tradition (scale 1); dark cases that might fit (scale 3); mythological traditions that might supply structure (scale 4). For each that is relevant, propose specific analogues.

2. **Open a conversation about scale 2.** Not formal questions — invitations to specify habits, observations, tics. Pull observed detail from the user's life. Signal that texture comes from observed life and chosen-against-template.

3. **Cross-check all proposed borrowings against the writer-persona's credible knowledge.** Adjust persona, schedule research, or pick different analogues as needed. Apply this *especially* sharply to non-Western mythological traditions and to specific case studies of human extremity.

4. **Decide on the explicitness mode** for each borrowing:
   - *Subtle* (reader who knows the source feels resonance; reader who doesn't simply finds it plausible)
   - *Visible to literate reader* (specific names or events recognisable to those with relevant knowledge)
   - *Declared* (author's note, dedication, in-text reference)

5. **For each borrowing, decide the twist.** What is being kept from the source; what is being shifted. Document this so the writer does not drift back toward source-fidelity during writing.

6. **Document everything in `notes/reality-grounding.md`** in the project. One section per scale. The borrowings, the depths, the twists, the texture-elements that will carry them.

7. **Reference this file during writing.** Each chapter's self-critique pass should include: "Where in this chapter does texture come from observed life or specific borrowing rather than genre-template? Have I chosen against the template at least once per scene?"

## A note on the master discipline

All four scales serve the same craft principle: **use real materials, then choose against the templates that AI generation produces by default.**

This is the single most important sentence in this document. The borrowing does not, by itself, save fiction from the AI-template feel. A borrowing that is averaged into the genre-mean of borrowings is still a template. The discipline is the *choice against* — picking the specific, the dry, the off-key, the structurally-borrowed-while-iconographically-invented, the case-specific banality instead of the theatrical menace, the character-specific habit instead of the evocative-generic detail.

Real writers do this without naming it. They have read and written enough that choosing-against-template is part of their working sense. AI-assisted writers must do it deliberately, scene by scene, sentence by sentence, because the system they are working with defaults to the average of everything it has read.

The discipline is unglamorous. It does not produce spectacular prose. It produces prose that *lands* — that the reader feels as true rather than recognising as competent. That is the goal of reality grounding at all four scales.
