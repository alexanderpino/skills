# Reading level — writing for the actual reader

## Why this discipline exists

Every book is written for a reader, but the reader's reading capacity is not optional context — it is the **constitutive constraint** that shapes everything else. Sentence length, vocabulary, conceptual density, how much background knowledge is assumed, how often new ideas can be introduced, how abstract a passage may go before it must return to concrete: all of this is determined by who can actually read what is on the page.

A six-year-old learning to decode words cannot read what a fourteen-year-old can read, who cannot read what an academic specialist can read. These are not three points on a single dimension; they are different territories of writing, each with its own craft, its own anti-AI-tells, its own exemplary authors. A persona designed to write for one cannot simply *try harder* to write for another. The voice, the reflexes, the discipline are different.

This reference exists because the skill, until now, has implicitly assumed a literate adult audience for every project. That assumption holds for most projects most users will pursue. But it fails immediately when the project is a children's book, a young-adult novel, an academic monograph, or a graded reader for second-language learners. Without explicit reading-level discipline, AI generation defaults to the smoothed adult-literary register regardless of who the book is for, and the result reads as a book *by* an adult *for* an adult dressed up to look like something else.

## The general principle

Reading level operates on five distinct dimensions:

**1. Decoding capacity.** Can the reader convert the printed words into language? Measured by AVI in Dutch, Lexile in English, CEFR in second-language acquisition. Operates at word and sentence level: how many syllables per word, how many words per sentence, how often complex spelling patterns appear, how much white space supports the eye.

**2. Comprehension capacity.** Even when decoding is fluent, can the reader follow what the sentences say? Measured by CLIB in Dutch, supports Lexile, B1/B2/C1/C2 in CEFR. Operates at the level of subordinate clause depth, abstract reference, idiom density, knowledge of figurative language.

**3. Background knowledge.** What can be assumed without explanation? An eight-year-old needs *Hitler* introduced; a sixteen-year-old does not; a graduate student needs the historiographical debate around Hitler's leadership style sketched. Background-knowledge calibration is invisible when correct and obvious when wrong.

**4. Conceptual density.** How many new ideas per page? An AVI-Start book might introduce one new idea per spread; a Burleigh-level adult history might develop five or six analytical points per page; an academic monograph might compress fifteen. Reading level partly determines what density the reader can sustain before the book becomes either insulting or impenetrable.

**5. Attention span and pacing.** How long can the reader follow extended argument before needing a return to scene, example, or dialogue? An eight-year-old needs concrete return every paragraph; an academic reader can sustain a forty-page chapter of close argument.

These dimensions are independent. A child with high decoding capacity may have low background knowledge for adult topics. An adult with limited literacy may have rich life experience and complex comprehension. The skill must address each dimension separately when planning a project.

## The Western measurement systems

This reference uses three systems prominently because they are the most established in the user's likely context. Other systems exist (Spache, Flesch-Kincaid, Fry Graph, Reading Recovery) and serve specialised purposes.

### AVI (Dutch)

The Dutch AVI system, developed by Cito, has twelve levels keyed to the Dutch primary school years. Each level corresponds to a stage in technical reading ability, measured by speed and accuracy reading a calibrated text.

- **AVI-Start** (begin groep 3, age 6): just beginning to decode. Words spelled exactly as pronounced (*maan*, *roos*, *bos*). Each sentence on a new line. Maximum support from images.
- **AVI-M3** (mid groep 3, age 6-7): three-letter and short four-letter words, simple consonant clusters (*spin*, *rent*).
- **AVI-E3** (end groep 3, age 7): three-syllable words allowed, sentences slightly longer.
- **AVI-M4** (mid groep 4, age 7-8): some loanwords, complex spellings (*ie* for *i*, *c* as *k* or *s*).
- **AVI-E4** (end groep 4, age 8): sentences begin not always on new lines, simple compound sentences.
- **AVI-M5/E5** (groep 5, age 8-9): complex sentences allowed, harder loanwords (*gamen*, *ideeën*, *ruïne*).
- **AVI-M6/E6** (groep 6, age 9-10): unfamiliar words like *ov-pas*, *ornament*. Less white space, fewer illustrations.
- **AVI-M7/E7** (groep 7, age 10-11): no length restrictions, long words frequent.
- **AVI-Plus** (end groep 7 / groep 8, age 11-12): essentially unrestricted; reader can handle adult prose with effort.

Beyond AVI-Plus is adult reading, which the AVI system does not stratify further.

A separate Cito system, **CLIB**, measures *comprehension* (begrijpend lezen) on parallel levels (CLIB-Start through CLIB-Plus). A child can have high AVI and low CLIB, or vice versa. Books for children should be chosen on both axes.

### Lexile (English)

The Lexile Framework rates texts on a single scale from below 0L (simplest) to about 1700L (highly complex), with most adult texts in the 1000-1300L range. The system is widely used in US schools and increasingly internationally for English texts.

Approximate Lexile ranges by US grade level:
- Kindergarten / Grade 1: BR (beginning reader) to 230L
- Grade 2: 230-580L
- Grade 3: 360-720L
- Grade 4: 415-780L
- Grade 5: 580-830L
- Grade 6: 690-925L
- Grade 7: 730-985L
- Grade 8: 770-1010L
- High School: 870-1185L
- College/Adult: 1185L and above

Newspaper journalism typically scores around 1100-1300L. *Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone* is around 880L. *To Kill a Mockingbird* is 870L. *The Great Gatsby* is 1070L. Academic prose often exceeds 1400L.

### CEFR (second-language)

The Common European Framework of Reference for Languages classifies second-language proficiency in six levels:

- **A1** (beginner): can understand familiar everyday expressions, very basic phrases.
- **A2** (elementary): can understand sentences and frequently used expressions on immediate concerns.
- **B1** (intermediate): can understand main points of clear standard input on familiar matters.
- **B2** (upper-intermediate): can understand main ideas of complex texts on concrete and abstract topics.
- **C1** (advanced): can understand a wide range of demanding, longer texts and recognise implicit meaning.
- **C2** (proficient): can understand virtually everything heard or read.

For graded readers and language-learning materials, CEFR is the standard. For adult literature in a second language, B2-C1 is typically required.

### What these systems measure and what they don't

All three systems measure **decoding and surface comprehension**. None measures emotional readiness, cultural knowledge, or thematic complexity. A book may be technically AVI-M5 but deal with grief in a way that requires emotional maturity beyond an eight-year-old. A book may be Lexile 1100L but assume cultural knowledge a non-native reader lacks. The systems are starting points, not endpoints.

The skill should use these systems as anchors during intake (so the user has a precise target) and as discipline during writing (so AI does not drift toward its smoothed adult-literary default), but should also assess emotional and cultural fit independently.

## Reading level categories — craft per level

For each category below: what the level can sustain, what AI tends to do wrong, exemplary authors as anchor points.

### Early decoding (AVI-Start to E3 / Lexile BR-360L / CEFR A1) — ages 5-7

**What the level can sustain.** Single-clause sentences. Words of one or two syllables. Vocabulary from the child's spoken life. Repetition of structures across pages (rhythmic patterning is helpful, not boring, at this level). Pictures carry equal narrative weight to text. New ideas at most one per page-spread.

**What AI tends to do wrong.**
- *Bevoogding* (condescension): AI writes for children rather than from the same imaginative space as children. Marker phrases: "Wat denk jij dat...", "Kijk eens wat...", "Wist je dat...". Children's writers do not interrupt to check that the child is following.
- *Adult themes lightly disguised:* AI writes "De man was triest" expecting that the simple sentence makes the adult-emotional content child-appropriate. It does not. Annie M.G. Schmidt's people are sad in ways children recognise from their own life, not in adult-emotional ways simplified.
- *Moralising:* AI tends toward the lesson-at-the-end. Real children's books resist this; the meaning emerges from the events, not from a closing observation by a narrator.
- *Vocabulary creep:* AI inserts an unusual word "for richness." A six-year-old does not need richness. They need every word recognisable.

**Exemplary authors (Dutch).** Annie M.G. Schmidt (*Pluk van de Petteflet*, *Jip en Janneke* — though *Jip en Janneke* is even simpler, AVI-Start territory). Dick Bruna (the Nijntje books — extreme economy, specific colour palette, square format). Joke van Leeuwen (later years). Imme Dros (early decoders).

**Exemplary authors (English).** Dr. Seuss (technical mastery in extreme constraints). Maurice Sendak (*Where the Wild Things Are* — emotional truth in 338 words). Mo Willems (the Elephant and Piggie series).

**Persona requirements.** A persona for this level must be someone who *thinks* in this register, not someone who can dial down. Schmidt did not write children's books because she could not write adult literature; she wrote them because the children's-book imagination is what she had. A skill-user wanting to write at this level should specify a persona modelled on a real children's-author profile, not adapt Daan Verstegen.

### Confident decoding (AVI-M4 to E5 / Lexile 360-830L / CEFR A2-B1) — ages 7-9

**What the level can sustain.** Two-clause sentences. Some loanwords with explanation. Multi-paragraph chapters. Plot becomes carrier of meaning; theme stays concrete. Dialogue can show character. Internal monologue stays simple. Concepts beyond immediate experience can be introduced if grounded in concrete example.

**What AI tends to do wrong.**
- *Premature complexity:* sentence-length control slips, three-clause sentences appear, vocabulary widens uncontrolled.
- *False simplification:* the structure stays simple but adult-emotional or adult-political content shows through.
- *Generic adventure:* AI defaults to a quest plot with stakes-and-resolution, missing the small specific texture that makes Jacques Vriens or Roald Dahl work.

**Exemplary authors (Dutch).** Jacques Vriens (*Achtste-groepers huilen niet*). Carry Slee (lighter early work). Tonke Dragt (early novels — *De brief voor de koning* is on the upper edge).

**Exemplary authors (English).** Roald Dahl (master of the level — moral darkness inside accessible prose). E.B. White (*Charlotte's Web*). Beverly Cleary.

### Advanced child / pre-young-adult (AVI-M6 to Plus / Lexile 700-1000L / CEFR B1-B2) — ages 9-12

**What the level can sustain.** Most published children's literature operates here. Sentence length and vocabulary approach adult prose. Plot can be intricate, multiple characters with their own arcs. Themes can be heavy (death, injustice, family conflict) but typically resolve toward hope. Background knowledge is built in-text rather than assumed.

**What AI tends to do wrong.**
- *YA-creep:* AI mistakes this level for young-adult and inserts romance, identity-crisis, or social-issue material that fits older books but breaks the implicit contract with this age.
- *Excess interiority:* the level does not sustain extended internal monologue at the rate adult literary fiction does. AI tends to over-internalise.
- *Lesson-driven plot:* especially common when the AI knows the topic is "important" (war, slavery, environment). The plot becomes a vehicle for the lesson rather than a story that has its own logic.

**Exemplary authors (Dutch).** Tonke Dragt (*De brief voor de koning*, *De Zevensprong*). Paul Biegel. Jan Terlouw (*Oorlogswinter*, *Koning van Katoren*). Joke van Leeuwen (later work).

**Exemplary authors (English).** Philip Pullman (*His Dark Materials* — upper edge of this level). Katherine Paterson (*Bridge to Terabithia*). Roald Dahl's longer works (*The Witches*, *Matilda*).

### Young Adult (Lexile 800-1100L / CEFR B2) — ages 13-17

**What the level can sustain.** Adult prose constraints largely lifted. Difference from adult fiction is thematic and tonal, not technical. Protagonists typically teen-age, conflicts identity-driven, romance and sexuality often present, social-issue content acceptable. First person common but not required.

**What AI tends to do wrong.**
- *Adult-pretending-to-be-teen voice:* AI writes what an adult thinks teens sound like rather than what teens actually sound like. Markers: explanatory dialogue ("As you know, my dad has been distant since the divorce..."), cultural references that signal effort.
- *Over-explained emotion:* teens in real life narrate feeling tersely or not at all; AI overwrites.
- *Issue-tourism:* AI can drift toward treating teen experience as a vehicle for adult social commentary about teen life.

**Exemplary authors.** John Green (US — *The Fault in Our Stars*). Carry Slee (Dutch — *Spijt*). Sarah Dessen. Markus Zusak (*The Book Thief* — upper edge).

### Adult literary general (Lexile 1000-1300L / CEFR C1) — adult readers without specialist training

**What the level can sustain.** This is the default register the skill has been operating in. Daan Verstegen, Marten Holvast, Saskia Vermeer all write here. Sentences 15-30 words, abstract concepts allowed, cultural references assumed within the author's milieu, multi-page argument acceptable.

**What AI tends to do wrong.** This is where the existing anti-AI-tells and language-naturalness disciplines apply most directly.

**Exemplary authors.** Mak (Dutch journalism-history). Burleigh, Kershaw, Tooze (anglophone history). Knausgård (autobiographical fiction). McCarthy (literary fiction). Le Guin (speculative).

### Adult specialist / academic (Lexile 1300L+ / CEFR C2) — disciplinary readers

**What the level can sustain.** Long sentences, technical vocabulary, theoretical apparatus, footnoted historiographical debate, full disciplinary jargon, references to ongoing debates without exposition. Reader is assumed to know the field.

**What AI tends to do wrong.**
- *Fake-academic stance:* AI writes "It has been argued that..." constructions without specifying who. Real academics specify: "Wehler argues...", "the Sonderweg thesis posits...", "Browning has shown that...".
- *Hedge-stacking as expertise marker:* "It might perhaps be argued that, in some sense, this could potentially..." — AI mistakes hedging for academic rigour. Real academics commit clearly and then specify the limits.
- *Citation-tourism:* AI inserts citations that look right but doesn't engage the cited work. Real academics either engage the citation substantively or leave it out.
- *Synthetic neutrality:* AI writes as though all positions deserve equal weight. Real academics have positions.

**Exemplary authors.** Within history: Wehler, Mommsen, Friedländer (German tradition); Hobsbawm, Hill, Thompson (British). In philosophy: full register depends on subdiscipline. In sciences: depends entirely on field.

### Specialised literacy registers (any Lexile, any age)

A few cases worth flagging:

- **Graded readers for second-language learning.** Constrained vocabulary lists, tightly controlled grammar progression, often paired with audio. CEFR-aligned. Different craft from native-language children's books — the constraints are systematic rather than developmental.
- **Adapted-text editions for adults with limited literacy.** *Boek-en-zo*-style or English equivalent. Adult themes, simplified syntax. Distinct craft territory; few writers do it well because it requires both adult sensibility and constrained vocabulary discipline.
- **Plain-language government and medical writing.** Specialised target reader, defined readability metrics, often regulated. Not literary writing but a real craft.

## How reading level integrates with persona

Not every persona can write at every level. A persona designed for adult literary general (Daan, Marten, Saskia) cannot simply be redirected to write children's books — the reflexes, the reading history, the rhythm preferences are wrong.

When a project's reading level differs from a persona's natural register, the skill must either:

1. **Build a new persona** suited to the reading level. A children's-book persona has read Schmidt and Bruna, has spent time around children, has a particular relationship to image-text balance. A YA persona has read Carry Slee, John Green, Markus Zusak; has retained access to adolescent register without condescension.
2. **Reframe the project** so the existing persona's natural register is appropriate. A children's-book project written by a Daan-type persona will fail; if the user wants to keep Daan, the project needs to become an adult book.

This is a real Phase 0 / Phase 1 decision. The skill should not let users sleepwalk into projects where persona and reading level mismatch.

The persona document should specify reading level explicitly. For Daan: "writes at adult literary general (Lexile ~1100L, CEFR C1) for Dutch-reading adults with secondary education or higher." For a children's-book persona: "writes at AVI-M5 / CLIB-M5 (age 8-9) for early independent readers."

## How reading level integrates with language-naturalness

The two disciplines work together but operate at different levels.

Language-naturalness asks: *would a real writer in this language write this sentence in this form?* It addresses syntactic and idiomatic naturalness against the AI-default.

Reading level asks: *is this sentence appropriate for the intended reader's capacity?* It addresses whether the sentence belongs in this book at all, regardless of how natural it is.

A sentence can be language-natural but reading-level wrong (a perfectly Dutch adult sentence in a children's book). A sentence can be reading-level appropriate but language-unnatural (a children's-book sentence with anglicism syntax). Both checks must run.

For each level, the relevant language-naturalness patterns also need adjustment. Children's writers do not use *toch* the way Daan does. YA writers use different insertion words. Academic writers have their own register markers. The persona document should specify both reading level and the corresponding language-naturalness profile.

## What the skill should do during Phase 1 (Intake)

The intake conversation must include explicit reading-level scoping. Add to the intake questions:

> **Reading level / target reader:** What is the reader's reading capacity? Options:
> - **Early decoding** (children learning to read, age 5-7, AVI-Start to E3)
> - **Confident decoding** (independent young readers, age 7-9, AVI-M4 to E5)
> - **Advanced child / pre-YA** (age 9-12, AVI-M6 to Plus)
> - **Young adult** (age 13-17)
> - **Adult literary general** (default for most projects — secondary-educated adults)
> - **Adult specialist / academic** (disciplinary readers in a field)
> - **Second-language learner at level X** (CEFR A1, A2, B1, B2, C1, C2)
> - **Adapted-text for adults with limited literacy**
> - **Other / mixed** (let me describe)

Cross-check the answer with the persona. If they mismatch, return to Phase 0 to adjust persona before proceeding.

## What the skill should do during Phase 4 (Writing)

For each chapter, the self-critique pass adds a reading-level check:

- Is sentence length within the level's tolerance? (Specific numbers depend on level — for AVI-M3 maximum 8 words, for AVI-Plus 25 words, for academic 40+.)
- Is vocabulary within the level's range? (Specific tests depend on level — for AVI a Cito-aligned word frequency check; for Lexile a text-analysis tool.)
- Are concepts introduced at the right rate? (Depends on level — children's books one new concept per spread, academic prose can stack.)
- Are cultural and background references appropriate? (Children: assume only what they know. Adult specialist: assume disciplinary literacy.)
- For per-level anti-AI-tells: see the relevant section above.

This check runs alongside the existing anti-AI-tells and language-naturalness checks during per-paragraph self-critique.

## What the skill should do during Phase 5 (Manuscript Finishing)

A reading-level revision pass should be added between the language-naturalness pass and the copy-edit pass. Read the whole manuscript with the explicit question: *would the intended reader actually be able to read this?* Tools that produce automated readability scores (AVI for Dutch, Lexile for English) can be run as a sanity check on whole chapters or chapter samples. Manual review remains essential — readability scores miss the conceptual and cultural dimensions.

If the manuscript scores significantly above or below the intended reading level, this is not a copy-edit issue but a craft issue. Parts of the manuscript may need to be rewritten at the appropriate level. This is why the pass exists in Phase 5 — late enough that the book is complete, early enough that revision is still possible.

## A note on respect

The hardest discipline in writing for any reading level below adult-literary-general is respect. The temptation is to condescend: to simplify because we think the reader cannot handle complexity, to moralise because we think they need lessons, to over-explain because we doubt their attention. The greatest writers at every level resist these temptations. Schmidt's children are full people. Pullman's adolescents are full people. Plain-language writers for adults with limited literacy treat their readers as full people.

The skill's purpose at every reading level is to make books that respect the reader. The disciplines in this reference exist to serve that purpose. They are not ends in themselves.
