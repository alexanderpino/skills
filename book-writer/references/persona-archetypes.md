# Persona archetypes by genre

When the user chooses Route C (archetypes) in Phase 0, offer 2–3 of these as starting points. The user picks one and then customises. Archetypes are not finished personas — they're scaffolding the user fleshes out.

Don't present these as "the options". Pick the two or three that best suit the specific book the user has in mind, and frame them as "here are some starting points, pick one to build from — or we can go another route."

---

## For textbooks

### The patient teacher
Mid-career academic who chose teaching over the research arms race. Knows the field deeply but would rather build intuition than show off. Warm, unhurried, willing to say "I found this confusing myself for years." Rare exclamation, no jokes. Trusts the reader's intelligence but not their patience.

### The enthusiastic practitioner
Someone who came to the field from outside — an engineer who fell in love with theory, a linguist who became a computer scientist. Writes with the freshness of someone who still remembers what it was like not to know. Conversational. Uses analogies freely. First-person natural.

### The rigourous formalist
Older, more traditional. Writes in authorial "we". Believes the formal structure of the field is beautiful and that the reader will agree if shown clearly. Measured, precise, patient with notation. Dry but not cold. Not for every audience — good for advanced undergraduates who want the real thing.

## For academic (research-level) books

### The senior researcher
20+ years in the field, has seen fashions come and go. Writes with the long view. Acknowledges the history of ideas. Careful about claims the field hasn't settled. Measured, occasionally dry, no performance. Uses "we" or third person; first-person rare and weighted.

### The young synthesizer
Post-PhD, first major book. Intense command of the recent literature but still writes with the energy of someone who's still figuring it out. Brings fresh framings. Willing to take positions older authors would hedge. Careful with sources because their reputation is still being built.

### The applied rigorist
Lives between academia and industry. Cares about the formalism but equally about when it fails in practice. Uses more concrete examples than a pure theorist would. Will criticise their own field's blind spots. Direct.

## For popular-technical (Dummies, O'Reilly-trade, Pragmatic)

### The craftsperson-practitioner
Someone who has built the things they're writing about. Writes with the authority of someone who's debugged it at 2am. Direct, occasionally self-deprecating, allergic to marketing language. First-person natural. Small jokes. Warm toward the reader but no patience for corporate buzzwords.

### The gentle onboarder
Has taught themselves things repeatedly and remembers the friction. Writes for the person who opened the book nervous. Generous with encouragement, never condescending. Defines terms the first time without making a fuss. Humor is warm, not edgy.

### The opinionated sysadmin
Older, seen a lot of tech fads die. Strong opinions about the right way to do things, defended with reasons. Dry humor. Occasional grumpy aside. Knows when to be rigorous and when to wave their hands. The reader trusts them because they've clearly been burned by bad choices before.

## For biography

### The empathetic historian
Academically trained but writes for the general reader. Cares about the subject as a person, not just a historical figure. Scrupulous with sources but willing to reconstruct atmosphere from them. Recedes from the text; the subject is the star. First-person rare, used at method-note moments.

### The sympathetic journalist
Background in long-form journalism. Brings pace and scene-craft. More willing than an academic to use cinematic openings. Interviews where possible, uses living memory. Archive-competent but not archive-only. Voice present in selection and framing, rarely on the surface.

### The partisan insider
Writes about someone in their own field — a scientist writing a scientist's biography, a musician writing a musician's. Has access to technical detail a generalist biographer couldn't match. Risk: sympathy bias. Good partisan-insiders are explicit about their closeness to the subject and its limits.

## For narrative history

### The cinematic storyteller
Thinks in scenes. Opens chapters on specific days, specific weather. Research is deep but the writing hides the scaffolding. Measured sentences, strong verbs, concrete nouns. Dramatic irony used well. Footnotes are honest about what's reconstructed vs recorded.

### The big-picture analyst
More interested in forces than in scenes, though uses scenes to illustrate. The Barbara Tuchman register — authoritative, long view, unafraid of judgment. Occasional sweeping sentence that earns itself. Older register generally.

### The corrective revisionist
Comes to a well-trodden period to push back on the received story. Animated by a specific argument. Combative without being shrill. Willing to say "most accounts of this get it wrong" and back it up. Needs very tight sourcing — the corrective works only if watertight.

## For school textbooks

### The warm teacher
Twenty years in classrooms. Knows where students stumble because she's seen it happen. Patient, clear, without condescension. Uses examples from students' lives when they genuinely help, ignores trendiness. No forced humor — warmth instead.

### The clear explainer
Younger, often recently out of university. Remembers being a student. Writes with the clarity of someone who still remembers which parts were hard. Less history, more "here's how you'd actually think about it."

### The subject enthusiast
Loves the subject more than the pedagogy. Lets the subject's inherent interest pull the student along. Works best for subjects with genuine drama (history, biology, geography, physics). Can over-assume reader enthusiasm if not careful.

## For self-improvement / popular psychology

### The practitioner-coach
Professional coach or consultant, typically 15-25 years in practice. Writes from direct client work — anonymised case studies, patterns they've seen recur. Warm but not fluffy. Respects the reader's intelligence, doesn't oversell. Uses frameworks because frameworks work, not because frameworks sell.

### The evidence-based skeptic
Psychology or behavioural science background. Specifically cares about what the research actually shows versus what pop-psychology claims. Cites studies but also notes when findings are weak. A useful correction to the guru voice.

### The recovered sufferer
Writes about something they struggled with themselves and worked through. The authority is experience rather than credentials. Honest about their own failures. Most trustworthy for topics where lived experience is the strongest teacher (addiction, grief, burnout, chronic illness).

### The synthesist
Not a specialist — a generalist who reads across disciplines (behavioural economics, neuroscience, philosophy, ancient wisdom) and finds the common threads. Works when done rigorously; fails when done sloppily. Requires more research than the others.

## For essay collections

### The observer-essayist
The Joan Didion type. Precise prose, moral attention, interest in how things feel as much as what they are. Writes as if noticing things other people missed. Long sentences sometimes; short, declarative ones often. A voice distinct enough that the reader would recognise a paragraph without the byline.

### The cultural critic
Writes in response — to a book, a film, a person, a moment. Less autobiographical than the observer-essayist. Uses the occasion of writing about something specific to open onto something larger. Argumentative when needed; curious by default.

### The writer-as-thinker
Essays as the record of thinking through something. The reader watches the thought develop. Less concerned with arriving at a conclusion than with showing the work. Intellectual warmth. Willing to be uncertain on the page.

## For memoir — a special note

Memoir is the genre where the "fictional persona" system from `writer-persona.md` does *not* apply in its normal form. The author of a memoir is the author — a real person writing their real experience. Archetypes don't fit here because the reader will know who wrote the book.

**What the skill should do for memoir instead:**

1. Skip the fictional persona intake entirely.
2. Instead, invite the user to articulate their own voice — not construct one. Questions like: "How would a close friend describe how you talk? What do you sound like when you're most yourself on the page? What do you avoid saying, even in private?"
3. Build a `persona.md` that describes the real author's voice — not a character, but a portrait of their existing sensibility. Many of the same fields apply (hobbyhorses, how they handle uncertainty, what they avoid) but the framing is "this is how I write" not "this is who is writing."
4. The "under your real name" provision in `writer-persona.md` applies in full — anecdotes must be true, not invented.
5. If the user *wants* some authorial distance — a pen name, a lightly fictionalised voice — that's legitimate and can be built. But it's a conscious choice, not a default.

The outline and writing workflow for memoir is otherwise the same as any other non-fiction book, with the ethical constraints in `genre-profiles.md § 7` as binding rules.

## How to present archetypes to the user

Pick 2–3 that fit the genre and the specific book. Describe them in a couple of sentences each, without loading them with too many prescribed details. Example:

> "For a book teaching garbage collection to working programmers, I'd suggest:
>
> - **The craftsperson-practitioner** — someone who's built runtimes, writes with authority-earned-through-scars, direct and warm.
> - **The opinionated sysadmin** — older, grumpier, strong views on why most GCs are designed badly, funny in a dry way.
> - **The gentle onboarder** — writes for the reader who's intimidated, patient and encouraging, lots of small examples.
>
> Pick one to build out, or let me propose something else."

Then when they choose, move into the structured intake (Route A) or distillation (Route B) to flesh out the specific person — name, age, background, hobbyhorses, formative experiences. The archetype is the seed; the finished persona is a real individual.

---

## Combining archetypes

Some books call for mixes. "The craftsperson-practitioner, but with the long view of a senior researcher" is a legitimate ask. If the user describes a hybrid, build it from both — don't force them to pick one.
