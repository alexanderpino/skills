# Extreme constraint — writing in the Bruna register

## Why this reference exists

The reading-level reference identifies *Early decoding* (AVI-Start to E3, ages 5-7) as one of the categories the skill must support. But that category contains an even more extreme sub-territory: the picture book for the youngest readers, where every constraint operates simultaneously and most published work is shallow precisely because the constraints are so hard.

Dick Bruna's *Nijntje* books are the canonical example in Dutch. Sixteen-by-sixteen centimetres, twenty-four pages, twelve illustrations on the right-hand page, four lines of text in rhyme on the left-hand page. Rhyme scheme abcb. Words almost always one syllable. Vocabulary within what a four-year-old already speaks. And — this is the easily-missed part — emotional truth. Bruna's *Nijntje in het ziekenhuis* (1973) deals with anaesthesia, fear, missing your parents. *Lieve oma pluis* (1996) is about the death of a grandparent. Bruna does not avoid the heavy subjects; he reaches them with extreme economy.

Children's writers who match this register (Bruna, Schmidt at her most economical, Bruna's English equivalents like Margaret Wise Brown of *Goodnight Moon*) are doing the hardest work in any language. Eight thousand words of adult literary prose are easier to write well than ninety-six words across twenty-four pages of picture book.

For most projects this discipline is overkill. For projects that genuinely target the youngest readers — and especially for projects that want to do that on heavy subjects — it is constitutive.

The test case that makes this matter clear: **a Bruna-register book about the Second World War**. Not a watered-down history lesson. A real picture book for four-year-olds, with twelve illustrations, four lines of rhyme per spread, that does not lie about what happened and does not break the child.

This reference is for that kind of project.

## Persona archetype: the Bruna-register writer

A persona who can work in this register has specific traits. They are not "Daan Verstegen who writes simpler" or "Marten Holvast for kids". They are a specifically constructed person.

### Reading and craft formation

- Has read **Bruna across his whole oeuvre**, not just the famous Nijntjes. Knows that *Lieve oma pluis* exists. Knows that *het rode vogeltje* (1959) — Bruna's earliest dealing-with-loss book — predates the Nijntje style. Has paid specific attention to what Bruna *does not say*: the absent words.
- Has read **Margaret Wise Brown** (*Goodnight Moon*, *The Runaway Bunny*) and noticed the same economy in English.
- Has read **Maurice Sendak** (*Where the Wild Things Are* — only 338 words across the whole book, with the longest single sentence being "let the wild rumpus start"). Has noticed how Sendak handles the dark territories of childhood (anger, abandonment) without protective covering.
- Has read **Annie M.G. Schmidt** specifically *Jip en Janneke* (very short stories, 200-300 words each) — not the longer works.
- For heavy subjects: has read **Irène Cohen-Janca, *L'arbre d'Anne*** (translated as *De boom van Anne*) — a picture book about the chestnut tree outside Anne Frank's window, for ages 6+. Has read the Nederlandse 4-mei-traditie of small books for very young children about war and remembrance.

### Habits of mind

- **Comfortable with silence on the page.** Knows that an empty pause between two short sentences can do work that a long sentence cannot.
- **Resists the lesson.** Does not write picture books to teach. Writes them to be true. Has a specific allergy to the closing-moralising-paragraph that AI gravitates toward when topics feel important.
- **Trusts the illustration.** Knows the book is half pictures, and the picture-text division of labour is a real craft decision per spread.
- **Is patient with reduction.** Will spend a full day getting a four-line verse right, removing one word, replacing another. Treats the constraints as a craft, not as a limitation.
- **Has spent real time with children of the age.** Either has children, has worked with children, or has remembered being one with unusual clarity. Bruna said he still felt like a four-year-old in the way he looked at the world. This is not metaphor; it's an actual cognitive habit.

### What this persona refuses

- *Bevoogding*. Never asks the child rhetorical questions ("Wat denk jij dat...?"). Never explains what the child should feel.
- *Dressed-up adult content*. Never writes adult-emotional content with simple words. Knows the difference between a child being sad about a loss and an adult being sad about the same loss.
- *Lessons.* If the book has a moral, it lives in the events, not in any sentence.
- *Hopeful endings that do not match the events.* In *Lieve oma pluis*, oma stays dead. The book does not pretend otherwise. Honesty for the youngest reader is more important than comfort.
- *Excess vocabulary.* Will not insert an interesting word "for richness". Every word must be one a four-year-old already speaks.

### What this persona does

- Writes from one concrete image. Bruna does not write "about" loss; he writes about a particular small moment of the day after a death. The whole book is one image extended.
- Trusts repetition. The same phrasing returns, and that return is the work.
- Lets the picture carry the heavy. When a moment is too much for the words, the picture holds it. The text moves on.
- Ends in stillness, not resolution. The Bruna ending is rarely "and now it is fine." It is more often "and that is how it is."

## The discipline of extreme constraint

For any project in this register, the writing process is constrained at every level. Before drafting any text, all of these must be settled:

### Level 1 — Format

- **Trim size:** square 16x16 cm is canonical. Other small squares work. A landscape or portrait format breaks the convention.
- **Pages:** 24 is the Bruna standard. Could be 16 (shorter) or 32 (longer). Always a multiple of 4 (printer's signature requirement).
- **Picture / text division:** illustration on right, text on left is canonical. Other patterns (full-bleed picture with text below, every other spread silent) work but must be chosen and held.
- **Cover:** sturdy. Square. Image on front, sometimes spilling to back.

### Level 2 — Verse and meter

- **Verse form:** four lines per spread is canonical. Can vary (some Bruna books have two-line spreads; some have a few without text).
- **Rhyme scheme:** abcb (lines 2 and 4 rhyme). This is Bruna's standard. Other patterns (aabb, abab) work but the specific abcb has a reason: the unrhymed lines do narrative work, the rhymed lines do emotional work.
- **Meter:** consistent within the book. Three or four stresses per line is standard. The meter must be one a child can hear. Iambic patterns (te-DUM te-DUM) and trochaic (DUM-te DUM-te) both work; ballad meters work; free meter does not — it sounds wrong when read aloud.
- **Rhyming words:** must be in the child's spoken vocabulary. *Nijn rijmt op fijn en zijn, nooit op rolgordijn of zonneschijn* — Bruna's rule. The temptation to reach for an unusual rhyme is the death of this register.

### Level 3 — Sentence and word

- **Sentence length:** typically 4-8 words. Maximum 10 in extremis.
- **Word length:** one or two syllables. Three-syllable words possible but rare and only when the word is already in the child's vocabulary (*chocola*, *kangoeroe*).
- **Vocabulary:** approximately 200 different words across a whole book. Use spoken-Dutch frequency lists or similar resources for the target language. New words are introduced with picture support.
- **Tense:** simple present is most common in Dutch picture books. Past tense possible for narrative-distance ("toen was er een..."). Avoid complex tenses.
- **Pronouns:** specific characters named, not "she/he/they" until the character is anchored.

### Level 4 — Subject and tone

- **One image, extended.** Each book is built around one concrete image or moment. *Nijntje in het ziekenhuis* is one image: a small rabbit lying in a hospital bed. Everything in the book extends from that image.
- **Concrete throughout.** No abstractions. *Verdrietig zijn* shows the moment of being verdrietig, never explains it.
- **Emotional honesty.** Difficult feelings appear and are not protected from. They are also not dwelt on; the book moves through them at the rhythm of a four-year-old's experience.
- **No narrator commentary.** The narrator describes; the narrator does not interpret.
- **The ending.** Resolution at four-year-old scale (the child is held, the day ends, sleep comes) — not at adult scale (problem solved, lesson learned).

### Level 5 — Picture / text relationship

For each spread, decide: **what does the picture show, what does the text say, and where do they overlap or not?**

Three patterns:
- **Picture confirms text.** Text says "nijntje is op de fiets." Picture shows nijntje on a bicycle. Used most of the time. Anchors the child's understanding.
- **Picture exceeds text.** Text says nothing about the colour of the sky. Picture is brilliant blue. The picture is doing additional emotional work.
- **Picture and text point in different directions.** Rare and powerful. Text says one thing, picture suggests another. Used at climactic moments — the ending of *Lieve oma pluis* uses this. Reader (or read-aloud parent) must pause.

The skill should specify per spread which pattern applies.

## Pre-writing process for an extreme-constraint book

Before any verse is drafted, the following must be settled in writing:

1. **The one image.** What is the central image the book extends from? Stated in one sentence: "A small rabbit lying in a hospital bed." "A child looking out of a window at a tree." "A kitten that has lost its mother." This is the book's unmovable centre.

2. **The arc.** What happens across the 12 spreads? Three-act structure does not apply at this length; instead, a simple **before / during / after** or **away / encounter / home** structure usually works. Stated in 12 lines, one per spread.

3. **The vocabulary list.** ~150-200 words that the book will use. Drawn from the persona's understanding of the four-year-old's spoken vocabulary in the target language. The list is fixed before writing; verses must use only words from the list (with rare exceptions, justified).

4. **The rhyme constraints.** Which words will rhyme with which? Build a small rhyme-bank from the vocabulary list before writing. Most rhymes in a Bruna-style book come from a small set of words.

5. **The picture / text plan per spread.** For each of the 12 spreads: what does the picture show, what does the text say, what is the relationship?

6. **The ending.** Always written first, after the centre is decided. The ending is the book's commitment. If the ending does not work, the book will not work, and there is no point drafting twelve spreads to discover this.

## Anti-AI-tells specific to this register

AI generation in this register fails in specific ways. All of them must be checked.

### Tell 1 — Lesson at the end

AI gravitates toward closing the book with a stated meaning. *"En zo leerde nijntje dat..."*. Real picture books do not close on a stated meaning. The meaning is in the events.

### Tell 2 — Vocabulary creep

AI inserts a slightly elevated word "for richness". *"Een diepe stilte"* in a book that should say *"het was stil."* The instinct toward poetic enrichment is wrong here.

### Tell 3 — Adult-emotional content lightly disguised

AI writes "het was heel verdrietig" with simple words and thinks the simple syntax makes it a children's book. It does not. A child does not read "het was heel verdrietig" the way an adult reads "it was very sad". The child needs a concrete moment.

### Tell 4 — Question-asking narrator

AI inserts "wat zou er nu gebeuren?" or "wat denk jij?" thinking this engages the reader. Bruna does not do this. Schmidt does not do this. The narrator does not ask the child questions.

### Tell 5 — Comfort that does not match events

AI writes endings that resolve too cleanly. After difficult content, AI tends to add "maar het kwam helemaal goed" or equivalent. This is a lie when the events do not warrant it. *Lieve oma pluis* does not add comfort to oma's death.

### Tell 6 — Rhyme-driven content drift

AI selects content because it rhymes, not because it serves the story. The result is verse that scans but contains material that has no business in the book. The discipline: the content drives the verse, not the other way around. If a stanza's content is wrong, the rhyme must be discarded and rebuilt.

### Tell 7 — Generic kindness-language

AI uses words like *lief*, *fijn*, *blij* generically. Real picture-book writers use these words specifically — when something is fijn, the picture or the context tells us what made it fijn. The word does not float.

### Tell 8 — Picture-redundant text

AI writes text that says exactly what the picture shows. ("Nijntje heeft een rode jas. Op haar jas zit een knoop. De knoop is wit.") Real picture-book texts do less than this — they let the picture say "rode jas" and the text says something the picture cannot.

## What the skill should do for projects in this register

### Phase 0 (Persona)

Build the persona using the archetype above. The persona is **specific to the project**: an Bruna-register persona is not transferable to an adult-literary project, and vice versa. If the user is working on a project that requires this register, a new persona is built. The skill should not let users assume a Daan-equivalent persona can shift down.

### Phase 1 (Intake)

Reading-level scope is "Early decoding (AVI-Start to E3)". But also flag that this is the *extreme constraint* version of that level — the picture-book end, not the early-reader end. Confirm with the user that they want a real picture book, not a longer early-reader text.

### Phase 2 (Preparation)

The pre-writing settlements above (the one image, the arc, the vocabulary list, the rhyme constraints, the picture/text plan, the ending) are the Phase 2 deliverables. They take the place of the world-bible and character-bibles of fiction, and the source-list and chronology of non-fiction. Without them no verse should be drafted.

### Phase 4 (Writing)

Writing in this register is iterative at the verse level. Each spread (4 lines) gets multiple drafts before settling. The self-critique pass for each spread checks: meter, rhyme, vocabulary list compliance, picture/text relationship, the eight tells above. **The full book is approximately 96 words across 24 pages**; the skill must not be impatient with the time-per-word ratio. A full picture book may take as long as a chapter of an adult book.

### Phase 5 (Manuscript Finishing)

Read-aloud test. The book must be read aloud at least three times before being considered finished. Words that look right on paper sometimes fall wrong in the mouth. Meter problems often only emerge in reading aloud. Rhymes that look weak on paper may sound strong, and vice versa.

The reading-level pass uses an actual four-year-old's vocabulary as benchmark. If a word in the book is not one the writer can confidently say a four-year-old already knows, it must be replaced or supported by the picture so directly that the unfamiliar word is no obstacle.

### Phase 6 (Assembly)

Picture-book assembly is more demanding than prose-book assembly. The 12 illustrations are the larger half of the book; without them or detailed plans for them, the book cannot be assembled. For the skill, this means either AI-generated illustrations (with their limits), commissioned illustrations (out of scope for the skill itself), or detailed illustration briefs that an illustrator can execute.

For test or concept projects: detailed illustration briefs (one per spread) are acceptable as deliverable, with the understanding that the project is incomplete until illustrations exist.

## A note on heavy subjects

Picture books for the youngest readers can address heavy subjects. They have done so for as long as the form has existed. *Het rode vogeltje* is about death; *Lieve oma pluis* is about death; *Where the Wild Things Are* is about anger and abandonment; many less-famous picture books address divorce, illness, war, refugee experience, racism.

The discipline for heavy subjects in this register is the same as the discipline for any subject: one concrete image, four-year-old vocabulary, no lessons, emotional honesty, picture / text division of labour. The subject does not change the discipline. What changes is the responsibility — when the subject is a real historical evil, the writer carries the weight of treating it appropriately for a child who will not yet understand it cognitively but will register it emotionally.

For a Second World War picture book: do not treat the topic as an exception to the discipline. Treat it as a particularly demanding application of the discipline. The Bruna-register book does not depict atrocity; it depicts what one child experiences. Anne Frank's tree, seen from a window. A yellow star sewn on a coat. A train leaving a station. An empty chair at a table. Not the camp; never the camp. The everyday around it, which is what a child can absorb without breaking.

## A note on the limits of this register

Not every story can be told in this register. A book about the broad political mechanism of the Holocaust cannot be a Bruna-register book. A book about a particular small moment of one child's experience can be.

The skill's job is to help the user identify whether their project genuinely fits the register. If it does not, the skill should suggest alternative reading-level targets (advanced child / pre-YA, for example) where the constraints are looser and more of the story can be carried.

If the project does fit — if there is one image that can carry the whole book — then the discipline above applies, and the result will be hard to write but possible.
